INTERNSHIP SEARCH

Context & Problem Statement

When I started looking for an internship, I realized how much the “informal channel” matters: many opportunities get unlocked through recommendations, family, or someone already inside a company. For students without that advantage — which is common when you are new in a city/country (including many international students) — the process becomes more uncertain: fewer replies, longer delays, and less visibility.

Instead of taking it personally, I decided to treat it like a project: define a target, apply consistently, track results, and improve based on measurable feedback.

Aside: these notes are meant for people in the same situation — how to search seriously, without relying on a “network”.

Aside — Practical Method (repeatable)
1) Define a clear target
  • Role: pick 1–2 tracks max (Network / Sysadmin / Cybersecurity)
  • Duration & dates: write exact dates
  • Area: city + radius
  • What you can do: 3–5 concrete tasks (support, monitoring, documentation, basic hardening, diagnostics)
2) Track everything (so you can improve)

Create a simple table: company, date, channel, follow-ups, status, notes. Without data, you can’t identify what works and adjust your strategy.

3) Use follow-ups (professional, not pressure)
  • Follow-up #1: 3–5 days after sending
  • Follow-up #2: 7–10 days after follow-up #1
  • Then archive + move on (and re-contact later if needed)
4) Reduce uncertainty with a technical proof

A small, clean project helps: a lab (DNS/VPN/VLAN/pfSense/AD), a short report (1 page), or a GitHub README that shows: objective → steps → tests → results.

Evidence (screenshots)

The goal is not to expose private data, but to prove methodology: organization, consistency, and technical clarity.

Tip: insert screenshots right after the paragraph they support (tracking, message template, technical proof). Keep them anonymized and add a caption to show what the evidence proves.
